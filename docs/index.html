The Vessel

Built to Last Forever

RMS Titanic was the second of three Olympic-class ocean liners built by Harland and Wolff in Belfast for the White Star Line. At the time of her launch in 1911, she was the largest moving object ever built by human hands — a floating palace with a swimming pool, Turkish bath, and a first-class dining room that seated 554 guests.

Her hull was divided into 16 watertight compartments. Naval architects assured she could stay afloat with any four flooded. The iceberg opened five. Her fate was mathematically sealed within the first hour.

The Night

160 Minutes to the Bottom

April 14, 1912. The North Atlantic was unusually calm — ironically the worst condition for spotting icebergs, as waves breaking at their base would normally reveal them. Six iceberg warnings had been received by radio that day. Captain Smith kept full speed.

April 14  ·  11:40 PM
Iceberg sighted
Lookout Frederick Fleet rings three bells — "Iceberg, right ahead!" First Officer Murdoch orders hard-a-starboard. The ship begins to turn, but at 21 knots she needs more than half a mile to stop. She doesn't have it.
April 14  ·  11:50 PM
The damage is assessed
Architect Thomas Andrews inspects the lower decks. Five watertight compartments are breached — one more than she can survive with. He tells Captain Smith: she has at most two hours.
April 15  ·  12:05 AM
Lifeboats ordered uncovered
Captain Smith orders the crew to prepare lifeboats. There are 20 — enough for 1,178 people. There are 2,224 on board. No one yet believes the ship is truly sinking.
April 15  ·  12:15 AM
Distress signals sent
Radio operators Bride and Phillips transmit CQD then SOS. The Carpathia, 58 miles south-east, turns around at full speed. She will arrive in 90 minutes — 10 minutes too late for most.
April 15  ·  12:45 AM
First lifeboat lowered
Lifeboat 7 leaves with 28 of its 65 capacity. Passengers refuse to leave the warm, brightly-lit ship for a dark ocean. This pattern repeats for the next 80 minutes.
April 15  ·  2:20 AM
Titanic sinks
The stern rises high, the lights go dark, and the ship disappears beneath the surface. Over 1,500 people are in the 28 °F (−2 °C) water. Most will lose consciousness within minutes.
April 15  ·  4:10 AM
Carpathia arrives
RMS Carpathia reaches the lifeboats. 710 survivors are rescued. The search continues until it becomes clear there are none left to find.

The Kaggle Titanic dataset contains records for 891 of the ~2,224 aboard — a curated subset of the ship's manifest, augmented with ticket prices, cabin numbers, and family information. Each row is a person. The target column, Survived, tells us whether they made it.

What the data reveals

Survival Was Not Random

Three factors dominate survival in this dataset: sex, ticket class, and age. The "women and children first" maritime tradition was real — and the class divide was stark.

The starkest finding: a first-class woman had a 96.8% chance of survival. A third-class man had a 13.5% chance. Same ship, same iceberg — a sevenfold difference explained almost entirely by two categorical variables.

On the embarkation chart: Cherbourg passengers survived at a higher rate (55%) not because of any luck related to the port — but because more wealthy first-class passengers boarded there. Port of embarkation is a proxy for class, not a cause. This is the kind of confounding that EDA teaches you to spot before modelling.
